Rhode Skin:
How Hailey Bieber
Built a Billion-Dollar Brand

A strategic analysis of how a three-product skincare startup became a $1 billion brand through founder-led marketing, minimalist strategy, and cultural influence.

Pragnya Madiraju

Independent Case Study

Brand Strategy · Consumer Behavior · Digital Marketing

$1B acquisition by e.l.f. Beauty (2025)

Executive Summary

Rhode launched in June 2022 with only three products and became one of the fastest-growing beauty brands in the world. By 2025, it was acquired by e.l.f. Beauty in a deal worth up to $1 billion. Rhode achieved this through a combination of strong founder branding, minimalist product strategy, social media virality, community building, and disciplined product expansion.

Market Context

The Problem Rhode Solved

Before Rhode launched, the skincare market had become overwhelming. Consumers were faced with complicated multi-step routines, hundreds of competing products, ingredient overload, and confusing brand claims. At the same time, Gen Z consumers were increasingly seeking simpler routines, effective ingredients, clean aesthetics, and authentic recommendations.

Rhode positioned itself directly as the answer. Instead of adding to the noise, it launched with only three products — the Peptide Glazing Fluid, Barrier Restore Cream, and Peptide Lip Treatment — with a simple guiding message: Healthy skin first. Makeup second. This reduced consumer confusion and made purchase decisions effortless.

Key Insight

In a saturated market, radical simplicity becomes a competitive advantage. Rhode didn't compete on product volume — it competed on clarity of purpose.

Marketing Strategy

Four Pillars That Drove Growth

1
Founder-Led Marketing
Rather than using Hailey Bieber as a traditional spokesperson, Rhode made her the product demonstration. Morning skincare videos, GRWM content, and tutorials showed Rhode naturally integrated into her life. Consumers felt they were discovering products, not being sold to. Rhode transformed a celebrity into a living case study for the brand.
2
Trend Creation, Not Trend Following
Rhode popularized cultural moments like Glazed Donut Skin, Strawberry Girl Makeup, and Latte Makeup — trends that spread organically across TikTok and Instagram. Crucially, consumers wanted the exact products Hailey used, creating massive organic demand. Most brands chase trends. Rhode created them.
3
Strategic Scarcity
Rhode intentionally launched with very few products. This made decision-making easier, drove higher conversion rates, reduced inventory complexity, and built stronger hero products. Consumers quickly internalized: "If Rhode sells it, it must matter." Scarcity amplified perceived value.
4
Aesthetic Consistency
Across packaging, website, social media, and campaign photography, everything communicated minimalism, luxury, clean beauty, and modern femininity. The brand became instantly recognizable. Consumers weren't just buying skincare — they were buying into an aesthetic identity.

Consumer Behavior Insight

Rhode created strong social proof at every level. Posting Rhode products on social media wasn't just sharing a purchase — it was a signal of cultural participation. This turned customers into unpaid brand ambassadors.

Distribution Strategy

A Disciplined Two-Phase Approach

Phase 1 — Direct-to-Consumer: Rhode initially sold exclusively through its own website. This allowed the brand to capture higher margins, collect direct customer data, maintain full brand control, and build demand organically before expanding. Starting DTC was a deliberate choice, not a limitation.

Phase 2 — Sephora Expansion: After proving demand, Rhode expanded into Sephora — gaining greater credibility, enabling physical product testing, and dramatically increasing international reach. The move expanded accessibility while preserving brand prestige, because the demand had already been proven.

Distribution Lesson

Rhode's two-phase approach demonstrates how strategic patience in distribution creates stronger market entry. By building demand before expanding, the Sephora launch felt like a response to consumer pull rather than a push for sales.

SWOT Analysis

Evaluating Rhode's Position

Strengths
  • Massive founder influence and authentic reach
  • Strong, instantly recognizable visual identity
  • Viral organic social media presence
  • High customer loyalty and brand advocacy
Weaknesses
  • Heavy dependence on Hailey Bieber's personal brand
  • Limited product portfolio reduces revenue ceiling
  • Potential for trend fatigue over time
Opportunities
  • International market expansion
  • Growing Sephora footprint globally
  • Adjacent product categories (makeup, body care)
  • Men's skincare market entry
Threats
  • Highly competitive beauty market dynamics
  • Rapid shifts in social media trends
  • Celebrity brand saturation among consumers
  • Consumer preference shifts post-acquisition

Marketing Takeaways

Five Lessons from Rhode's Growth

1
Build a community before scaling products. Rhode's community came first. Products followed the demand it had already created.
2
Simple product lines can outperform large catalogs. Three hero products that consumers understand deeply beat fifty products nobody remembers.
3
Consumers buy identities, not products. Rhode sold simplicity, aspiration, and belonging — the skincare was the vehicle, not the destination.
4
Founder-led storytelling can be more effective than traditional advertising. Authentic integration into a founder's real life builds deeper trust than any campaign.
5
Creating cultural moments is more powerful than reacting to them. Rhode didn't wait for trends — it invented the vocabulary consumers used to talk about skincare.

Conclusion

What Rhode Really Sold

Rhode succeeded because it wasn't really selling skincare. It was selling simplicity, aspiration, community, and identity. The brand combined influencer power, disciplined product development, visual consistency, and social media strategy into a marketing engine that transformed a three-product startup into a billion-dollar brand in under three years.

The acquisition by e.l.f. Beauty validates the model — but Rhode's most valuable lesson isn't about celebrity or budget. It's about the power of knowing exactly what your brand stands for and refusing to dilute it.
